Section 01

Information Extraction (IE)

Definition Information Extraction (IE) is the task of automatically identifying and pulling out specific, structured facts from large amounts of unstructured text, so that a computer can store and use them easily.

Think about how much text exists in the world — news articles, doctor's reports, WhatsApp messages, legal documents, product reviews. Most of this text is unstructured, meaning it is written for humans, not machines. IE is the process of teaching computers to read this text and extract the useful pieces of information automatically.

Imagine you receive 1,000 job applications as plain text emails. Manually reading all of them to find each applicant's name, qualification, and experience would take days. An IE system reads all 1,000 emails in seconds and fills a table with those details automatically.

What does IE do?

IE converts unstructured text → structured data. For example:

Raw Text (Unstructured)Extracted Info (Structured)
"Rahul Sharma joined TCS in Pune on 3rd March 2023." Person: Rahul Sharma
Organization: TCS
Location: Pune
Date: 03-03-2023
"Patient Meena Gupta, 45F, was diagnosed with diabetes at AIIMS Delhi." Patient: Meena Gupta
Age: 45, Gender: F
Condition: Diabetes
Hospital: AIIMS Delhi

Architecture of an IE System

An IE system processes text in several ordered steps, called a pipeline:

IE System Pipeline — text flows left to right through each processing stage

Explanation of Each Step

  1. Raw Text Input: The system receives unstructured text — a news article, a report, a tweet, etc.
  2. Tokenization: The text is split into individual words or tokens.
    Example: "Riya visited Delhi" → ["Riya", "visited", "Delhi"]
  3. POS Tagging (Part-of-Speech): Each token is labelled as noun, verb, adjective, etc. This helps the system understand the grammar.
    Example: Riya/NOUN visited/VERB Delhi/NOUN
  4. Named Entity Recognition (NER): Specific tokens are classified as Person, Place, Organization, Date, etc. (detailed in Section 2).
  5. Relation Extraction: The system identifies how entities relate to each other.
    Example: Riya [VISITED] Delhi
  6. Structured Output: Results are stored in a table, database, or knowledge graph that computers can easily query.
⭐ Exam Tip The IE pipeline is a very common long-answer question (10–15 marks). Always draw/write it as a numbered flow from Raw Text → Structured Output and explain each step with an example.
Section 02

Entity Extraction & Named Entity Recognition (NER)

Definition — Entity Extraction Entity Extraction (also called Named Entity Recognition or NER) is the process of identifying and classifying real-world objects mentioned in text — such as people's names, place names, organization names, dates, and amounts — into pre-defined categories.

Types of Named Entities

Entity TypeWhat it representsIndian Examples
PERSONNames of peopleNarendra Modi, Virat Kohli, Priya Sharma
LOCATIONCities, states, countries, riversMumbai, Uttarakhand, Ganga, Himalaya
ORGANIZATIONCompanies, institutions, teamsISRO, Infosys, IIT Delhi, BCCI
DATE/TIMEDates, years, months15th August 1947, January 2024
MONEYCurrency amounts₹5,000, Rs. 2 crore
PERCENTAGEPercentage values7.5%, 12 percent

NER in Action — Visual Example

Riya visited Delhi in January to meet representatives from ISRO.
PERSON LOCATION ORGANIZATION DATE/TIME

Tata Motors announced a new plant in Pune on 12 April 2024 with an investment of ₹3,000 crore.

How Does NER Work?

Modern NER systems use machine learning (especially deep learning models like BERT) trained on thousands of labelled sentences. They learn patterns like:

  • Words after "Mr.", "Ms.", "Dr." are usually Person names.
  • Words after "in" or "at" are often Locations.
  • Words ending in "Ltd.", "Inc.", "Corp." are usually Organizations.
  • A four-digit number between 1900–2099 is likely a Year.
NER is like a highlighter in your textbook. If someone asks you to highlight all place names in a paragraph, you are doing NER manually. The computer does the same thing automatically across millions of pages.
⭐ Exam Tip NER is a Very High importance topic. In any long-answer question about NER: (1) give the definition, (2) list entity types with Indian examples, (3) show a labelled sentence, (4) mention applications in healthcare/social media.
Section 03

Phrase Extraction

Definition Phrase Extraction is the process of identifying meaningful multi-word groups (phrases) in a sentence that carry important information, as opposed to individual keywords.

A single word like "hackathon" tells you something. But the phrase "national level hackathon" tells you much more. Phrase extraction helps systems capture this richer meaning.

Types of Phrases

Phrase TypeAbbreviationExample
Noun PhraseNP"the new cricket stadium", "senior software engineer"
Verb PhraseVP"has announced", "will launch"
Prepositional PhrasePP"in New Delhi", "before the match"

Example — Classroom Activity

Activity Sentence: "The university organized a national level hackathon."

Important phrases extracted:
  • the university → Noun Phrase (who did it)
  • organized → Verb Phrase (the action)
  • a national level hackathon → Noun Phrase (what was organized)

Why is Phrase Extraction Important?

  • Search engines use it to understand multi-word queries like "best biryani in Hyderabad."
  • Summarization systems pick important phrases to create short summaries.
  • Question answering systems extract phrases as answers.
  • SEO (Search Engine Optimization) — website owners identify key phrases to rank better on Google.
When you read a chapter and underline important parts — not individual words but meaningful groups — you are doing phrase extraction. Computers do this systematically across millions of documents.
Section 04

Applications of Information Extraction

IE is one of the most practically useful areas of NLP. Below are the most important application domains with Indian-context examples:

1. Healthcare & Medicine

  • Extract patient names, diagnoses, medications, dosages from doctor's notes and prescriptions.
  • Hospitals like AIIMS use IE to convert handwritten reports into digital records.
  • Drug interaction databases are built by extracting information from research papers.

2. Social Media Monitoring

  • Extract product mentions, sentiments, event names from Twitter/X, Instagram, Facebook.
  • Political parties monitor public opinion by extracting entities and sentiments from crores of tweets.
  • Disaster management — during floods in Uttarakhand or Chennai, IE can extract locations, casualty numbers, road names from news feeds in real time.

3. Banking & Finance

  • Extract company names, transaction dates, amounts from financial news to power trading algorithms.
  • Banks use IE to process loan applications — automatically reading and extracting data from uploaded documents.
  • Fraud detection by extracting suspicious patterns from transaction descriptions.

4. E-Commerce

  • Flipkart and Amazon use IE to extract product attributes (brand, size, colour, price) from seller descriptions.
  • Customer review analysis — extract opinions about specific product features.
  • Automatic catalogue creation from unstructured product listings.

5. Legal & Government

  • Extract relevant clauses, parties, and dates from legal contracts.
  • Government schemes: automatically extracting beneficiary details from application forms.

Search Engines — History & Working

Definition A Search Engine is a software system that searches a large collection of documents (usually web pages) for content that matches a user's query, and returns a ranked list of relevant results.

Early Search Engines — Brief History

YearEngineWhat it did
1990ArchieFirst search tool — only searched FTP file names, no web pages.
1993Gopher / VeronicaSearched text menus (Gopher system), not the modern web.
1994Yahoo!Human-edited directory — people categorized websites manually.
1994AltaVistaFirst to crawl and index the full text of web pages automatically.
1996Ask JeevesAllowed natural language questions instead of keywords.
1998GoogleIntroduced PageRank — ranking pages by how many other pages link to them. Revolutionized search quality.

Today, Google handles over 8.5 billion searches per day worldwide. In India, Google holds over 97% of the search market.

How a Modern Search Engine Works

A search engine has three main parts: Crawler → Index → Query Processor

Search Engine Architecture

Step-by-Step Explanation

  1. Web Crawler (Spider/Bot): An automated program called a crawler or spider constantly visits web pages, reads their content, and follows all the links it finds to discover new pages. Google's crawler is called Googlebot.
  2. Parsing: The crawler extracts text, headings, keywords, and metadata (title, description, language) from each web page.
  3. Inverted Index: The extracted words are stored in a giant index. The system records which words appear in which documents. (Explained in detail in Section 6.)
  4. Query Processing: When you type a search query, the search engine looks it up in the index, finds matching pages, and ranks them.
  5. Ranking: Pages are ranked by relevance. Google uses 200+ factors including PageRank, content quality, and website speed.
  6. Viewing Results (SERP): The Search Engine Results Page (SERP) shows the ranked list with title, URL, and a short description (snippet) of each result.
Imagine Google is a library with 60 billion books. The crawler is a team of librarians who read every book. The index is the giant catalogue at the entrance. When you ask a question, the librarian checks the catalogue (not the books) — that is why the answer comes in milliseconds.

Section 06

Indexing — Text & Multimedia

Definition — Indexing Indexing is the process of organizing information from documents into a data structure that allows fast and efficient retrieval. It is the core reason why Google can search billions of pages in less than a second.

The Inverted Index — How Indexing Works

An inverted index maps each unique word to the list of documents (pages) where it appears. It is called "inverted" because it flips the normal document → word relationship into word → document.

Classroom Simulation Suppose we have 3 small documents:

Doc 1: "Cricket match in Delhi"
Doc 2: "Cricket fans in Mumbai"
Doc 3: "Football match in Mumbai"

The Inverted Index looks like this:
cricket
Doc 1, Doc 2
match
Doc 1, Doc 3
delhi
Doc 1
fans
Doc 2
mumbai
Doc 2, Doc 3
football
Doc 3

Now if a user searches for "cricket match", the system finds cricket → {Doc1, Doc2} and match → {Doc1, Doc3}. The intersection is Doc 1 — instantly, without reading any document again.

Why is Indexing Important?

  • Speed: Without indexing, every search query would have to read every single document — impossible at Google's scale.
  • Efficiency: Index lookup is thousands of times faster than full document scanning.
  • Scalability: The index can be updated incrementally as new pages are added.
An inverted index is exactly like the index at the back of a textbook. You look up "photosynthesis" → "pages 45, 67, 112". You go directly to those pages instead of reading the whole book. Google does the same at a scale of 60 billion pages.

Multimedia Indexing

Definition Multimedia Indexing is the process of indexing non-text content — images, audio files, and video files — so they can be searched and retrieved efficiently.

Text indexing works on words. But how do you index a photograph or a song? Multimedia indexing uses different techniques:

Media TypeIndexing TechniqueExample
Images 1. Text-based: Index surrounding text, alt-tags, captions
2. Content-based: Analyze colours, shapes, textures using AI 		Google Image Search, reverse image search
Audio 1. Speech-to-text conversion, then text indexing
2. Audio fingerprinting (unique signature) 		Shazam identifying songs, YouTube caption search
Video 1. Index metadata (title, description, tags)
2. Analyse keyframes as images
3. Transcribe audio track 		YouTube search, Netflix content search
Note Multimedia indexing is more complex than text indexing because computers cannot directly "read" images or audio the way they read text. Modern AI (CNNs, speech recognition models) makes it possible.

Queries & Searching

Definition — Query A query is the input (a word, phrase, or sentence) that a user provides to a search engine or information retrieval system to express what information they are looking for.

Query Processing Pipeline

Query Pre-processing Steps

  • Stop word removal: Common words like "the", "is", "a", "in" are removed as they add no search value. ("What is the capital of India?" → capital India)
  • Stemming: Words are reduced to their root form. ("running", "runs", "ran" → run)
  • Spell correction: Misspellings are automatically fixed. ("Mumai" → Mumbai)
  • Expansion: Synonyms may be added. ("automobile" also searches "car", "vehicle")

Searching Techniques

TechniqueHow it works
Sequential SearchReads every document one by one. Slow but accurate. Used for very small datasets.
Index-based SearchUses an inverted index for near-instant results. Used by all major search engines.
Fuzzy SearchFinds approximate matches. Useful for typos. ("Kolkota" returns results for "Kolkata")
Semantic SearchUnderstands the meaning, not just the exact words. ("big red fruit" might return results for "tomato" or "apple")
Full-Text SearchSearches every word in every document. Used in databases like MySQL, Elasticsearch.

Ranking — How Results are Ordered

After retrieval, documents must be ranked by relevance. Key factors:

  • TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency): A word appearing many times in a document (TF) but rarely across all documents (IDF) is considered highly relevant.
  • PageRank: Google's original algorithm — a page linked to by many other important pages is considered more trustworthy.
  • User signals: Click-through rate, time spent on page, bounce rate.
  • Freshness: Newer content is preferred for time-sensitive queries.
⭐ Exam Tip For queries, always mention: (1) definition, (2) at least 3 types with examples, (3) query pre-processing steps (stop words, stemming), (4) ranking factors (TF-IDF, PageRank). This comprehensively answers both 4-mark and 10-mark questions.
Section 08

Web Mining, Crawlers & Web Structure

Definition — Web Mining Web Mining is the application of data mining techniques to automatically discover and extract useful information from the World Wide Web — from web content, web structure, and web usage logs.

Three Types of Web Mining

TypeWhat it minesExample
Web Content Mining The actual text, images, and data on web pages Extracting product prices from Flipkart
Web Structure Mining The hyperlink structure between web pages (the "graph" of the web) PageRank — pages with more inbound links rank higher
Web Usage Mining Log files recording how users browse — which pages they visit, in what order Amazon recommending products based on your browsing history

Web Structure — How the Internet is Connected

The web is a giant directed graph:

  • Each web page is a node (vertex).
  • Each hyperlink from page A to page B is a directed edge A→B.
  • A page with many incoming links (in-degree) is considered important — this is the basis of PageRank.
Think of the web like a voting system. Every time a website links to another site, it is "voting" for it. A site that gets many votes (links) from reputable sites is considered important. This is exactly how Google's PageRank works.

Web Crawlers (Spiders)

Definition A Web Crawler (also called a spider or bot) is an automated program that browses the web systematically, following hyperlinks from page to page, to collect content for indexing.

How a Crawler Works

  1. Start with a seed list of known URLs (web addresses).
  2. Download the web page at each URL.
  3. Extract all hyperlinks from the downloaded page.
  4. Add new (unvisited) links to the queue.
  5. Repeat — this continues indefinitely, discovering new pages.
  6. Extracted content is sent to the indexing system.

Distributed Search & Crawlers

The web has over 1.9 billion websites. A single crawler would take years. Therefore, modern search engines use distributed crawling:

  • Thousands of crawler machines run in parallel across multiple data centres.
  • Google's data centres are spread across the USA, Europe, Asia (including India).
  • Each crawler machine is assigned a different portion of the web to crawl.
  • Results are collected centrally and merged into a single index.

Search Engine Coverage

Not all of the web is indexed by search engines. The web is divided into:

  • Surface Web (Indexed Web): Pages that search engines can find and index — approximately 4–5% of the total web. Includes Google-searchable content.
  • Deep Web: Pages not indexed by search engines — private databases, login-required pages, email inboxes, online banking portals. Not accessible via Google.
  • Dark Web: A small portion of the deep web that requires special software (like Tor) to access. Often associated with illegal activity.

Visualization in Web Mining

Visualization helps analysts understand patterns discovered by web mining:

  • Link graphs: Show how web pages are connected to each other.
  • Tag clouds: Visual representation of keyword frequency — more frequent words appear larger.
  • Heat maps: Show which parts of a webpage users click on most.
  • Traffic dashboards: Tools like Google Analytics visualize website visitor patterns.
Section 09

Summarization & Information Monitor

Automatic Text Summarization

Definition Automatic Text Summarization is the process of using a computer program to automatically create a shorter version of a text document that retains the most important information.

There are two main approaches:

Extractive SummarizationAbstractive Summarization
How it works Selects and copies the most important sentences directly from the original text Generates new sentences that capture the meaning (like a human would paraphrase)
Analogy Highlighting sentences in a book Writing your own summary in your own words
Complexity Simpler — no new language generation Complex — requires deep language understanding (GPT, BERT)
Example use News aggregators, legal document summaries Google's AI summaries, ChatGPT summaries

Training a Summarizer

A machine learning summarizer is trained on large datasets of (document, human-written summary) pairs. The model learns to predict which sentences or words are most important. Popular models include:

  • BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) — for extractive summarization.
  • T5, BART, GPT — for abstractive summarization.

Sentence Selection

In extractive summarization, the key challenge is sentence selection — deciding which sentences to include. Common scoring criteria:

  • Position: Sentences at the beginning and end of a document tend to be more important.
  • TF-IDF score: Sentences containing high-TF-IDF words are ranked higher.
  • Named entities: Sentences mentioning named entities (people, places) are often important.
  • Cue phrases: Sentences starting with "In conclusion", "Most importantly", "The key finding" are prioritized.
Example Original paragraph: "The Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) successfully launched the Chandrayaan-3 mission on July 14, 2023. The mission aimed to land near the lunar south pole. It made India the fourth country to achieve a soft landing on the Moon."

Extractive summary (1 sentence selected): "It made India the fourth country to achieve a soft landing on the Moon."

Information Monitor

Definition An Information Monitor (also called a Web Monitor or Change Monitor) is a system that continuously watches specified web sources and alerts the user when new or changed information matching their interest appears.

Examples of Information Monitors:

  • Google Alerts: You set up a keyword (e.g., "ISRO mission"), and Google emails you whenever a new article about it is published.
  • Stock price monitors: Alert you when a stock price crosses a threshold.
  • Social media monitors: Track brand mentions — companies are alerted whenever their product is mentioned on Twitter or Instagram.
  • News monitors: Journalists use tools like Mention.com to track news about specific topics.
  • Government portals: Monitor changes in government circulars or job notifications.
An information monitor is like setting up a Google Alert for your own name. Whenever your name appears anywhere on the internet, you get an email. The system is monitoring the web 24×7 on your behalf.

IE vs. Information Retrieval — Key Differences

This comparison is a High Probability exam question. Understand it clearly:

AspectInformation Extraction (IE)Information Retrieval (IR)
Goal Extract specific facts / structured data from text Find relevant documents that match a query
Output Structured data (table, database, JSON) List of relevant documents (search results)
Input Unstructured text User query + document collection
Processing Deep NLP: NER, relation extraction, parsing Index lookup + ranking
Example Extracting patient names and diagnoses from hospital records Google returning a list of relevant web pages for a search
What it answers "What specific facts are in this text?" "Which documents are relevant to my query?"
Tools/Tech SpaCy, NLTK, Stanford NLP, BERT Google, Elasticsearch, Apache Solr
Simple Analogy IR is like a librarian who says: "Here are 10 books about cricket."
IE is like a researcher who reads those 10 books and gives you: "Sachin Tendulkar scored 100 centuries. His first century was in 1990 against England."
