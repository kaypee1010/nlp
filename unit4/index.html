🚀 What is Text Mining?

Text Mining (also called Text Analytics) is the process of automatically extracting useful information, patterns, and knowledge from large amounts of unstructured text data.

Simple analogy: Imagine you have 10,000 customer reviews for a product. No human can read all of them. Text mining can automatically tell you: "70% reviews mention the battery life as a problem" — without anyone reading every review.

Structured vs. Unstructured Data

Structured DataUnstructured Text Data
FormTables, rows, columnsParagraphs, tweets, emails
ExampleExcel sales sheetNews articles, WhatsApp chats
Easy for computers?Yes ✓Very hard ✗
Volume in real world~20%~80%
Key insight: About 80% of all data in the world is unstructured text. Text Mining helps us unlock the value hidden in that 80%.

Origin of Text Mining

Text mining grew out of the field of Information Retrieval (how search engines work) in the 1990s, combined with ideas from Machine Learning and Linguistics. As the internet exploded with data, the need to automatically understand text became critical.

Objectives of Text Mining

① Extract useful facts and patterns from raw text
② Classify documents into categories automatically
③ Find relationships between entities (people, places, events)
④ Summarize large volumes of text
⑤ Enable intelligent search and question answering

🌍 Applications of Text Mining Very Important

1. Social Media Analysis

Mining tweets, Facebook posts, and Instagram captions to understand public opinion, trends, and emotions.

"Analysing 1 million tweets to find that 65% of people feel positive about a new government policy."

2. Spam Email Filtering

Gmail and Outlook use text mining to automatically detect spam. Words like "free money", "click here", "winner" in suspicious patterns get flagged.

3. Product Review Analysis

Amazon, Flipkart, and Zomato automatically process millions of reviews to generate star ratings and identify specific product issues.

4. Healthcare & Medical Records

Hospitals mine doctor's notes and medical reports to detect disease patterns, drug side effects, and predict patient risks.

5. Chatbots & Virtual Assistants

Siri, Alexa, and customer service bots use text mining to understand what a user is asking and find the right answer.

6. News & Media Monitoring

PR companies automatically monitor thousands of news articles to track how their brand is being discussed.

🧩 Understanding Text — Key Concepts

What is a Corpus?

A corpus is a large collection of text documents used for analysis. Think of it as the raw material for text mining.

Example corpus: All articles published by The Times of India in 2024 → forms a corpus for news analysis.

Words and Sentences in NLP

Before any analysis, we must define how text is broken into units:

UnitDescriptionExample
CharacterSingle letter or symbol'h', 'e', 'l'
Token / WordSmallest meaningful unit"hello", "the", "NLP"
SentenceGroup of tokens ending with punctuation"The dog ran."
DocumentCollection of sentencesA news article, email
CorpusCollection of documentsAll emails in a company

Challenges in Understanding Text

• Ambiguity: "I saw her duck" — dodge or animal?
• Slang & abbreviations: "OMG", "lol", "gonna"
• Spelling errors: "recieve", "teh"
• Multiple languages: Hinglish ("kya haal hai bro?")
• Context-dependency: Sarcasm, irony, cultural references

⚙️ Text Preprocessing Steps

Raw text is messy. Before mining, we must clean and standardize it. These steps are called preprocessing.

Step 1 — Tokenization

Tokenization = Splitting text into individual units (tokens/words/sentences).

Input: "The quick brown fox jumps."
Output: ["The", "quick", "brown", "fox", "jumps"]

Sentence tokenization:
Input: "I love NLP. It is useful."
Output: ["I love NLP.", "It is useful."]

Step 2 — Stopword Removal

Stopwords are common words that carry little meaning: "the", "is", "a", "an", "in", "of". Removing them reduces noise and speeds up processing.

Before: ["The", "quick", "brown", "fox"]
After: ["quick", "brown", "fox"] ← "The" removed

Step 3 — Lowercasing

Convert all text to lowercase so "Apple" and "apple" are treated as the same word.

"Hello World NLP" → "hello world nlp"

Step 4 — Stemming

Stemming cuts words down to their root form (may not be a real word).

"running" → "run"
"connected" → "connect"
"studies" → "studi" ← crude cut, not a real word

Step 5 — Lemmatization

Lemmatization is smarter — it reduces words to their dictionary base form (always a real word).

"running" → "run"
"better" → "good"
"studies" → "study" ← correct real word

Stemming vs. Lemmatization

StemmingLemmatization
SpeedFasterSlower
OutputMay not be real wordAlways a real word
AccuracyLowerHigher
Use whenSpeed matters mostAccuracy matters most

Step 6 — TF-IDF (Feature Extraction)

TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) measures how important a word is in a document relative to the whole corpus.

TF = How often a word appears in THIS document
IDF = How rare the word is across ALL documents
TF-IDF = TF × IDF → High score = important & rare word
If "cricket" appears 5 times in article A, and in only 2 of 1000 documents:
TF = 5 / (total words in A)
IDF = log(1000 / 2) = log(500) ≈ 2.7
TF-IDF = TF × 2.7 ← high score → "cricket" is key topic of article A

🗂️ Document Indexing Very Important

Indexing is the process of creating a data structure (the index) that allows extremely fast searching over a large collection of documents.

Book analogy: Imagine reading an entire 500-page textbook to find every mention of "photosynthesis". OR you can go to the index at the back and instantly find pages 34, 87, 212. Search engines build exactly this kind of index — but for billions of web pages.

What is an Inverted Index?

The most important type of index in text mining is the inverted index — it maps each word to the list of documents it appears in.

Documents:
Doc 1: "cricket match was exciting"
Doc 2: "cricket bat is wooden"
Doc 3: "football match in stadium"

Inverted Index built:
"cricket" → [Doc 1, Doc 2]
"match" → [Doc 1, Doc 3]
"exciting" → [Doc 1]
"bat" → [Doc 2]
"football" → [Doc 3]

Query: "cricket" → Instantly returns: Doc 1, Doc 2 ✓

🏷️ POS Tagging — Parts of Speech Very Important

POS Tagging (Part-of-Speech Tagging) is the process of labeling each word in a sentence with its grammatical category — noun, verb, adjective, etc.

Why does it matter? The word "bank" can be a noun ("the bank is closed") or a verb ("he will bank the money"). POS tagging resolves this by looking at context.

Common POS Tags

TagFull NameExamples
NNNoun (singular)dog, city, book, Ravi
VBVerb (base form)run, eat, write, solve
JJAdjectivesmart, big, beautiful, quick
DTDeterminerthe, a, an, this, that
INPrepositionin, on, at, by, with
RBAdverbquickly, very, well, soon
VBDVerb past tenseran, ate, solved, wrote
NNSNoun (plural)dogs, cities, books

🔗 Hidden Markov Models (HMM) Very Important

An HMM is a statistical model used to predict sequences — where the next item in the sequence depends on the current state. It is called "hidden" because we can observe the outputs (words) but not the underlying states (POS tags) directly.

Real-Life Analogy — Weather States

Imagine you can't see outside but you can observe what a friend wears:
• If they wear a raincoat → probably Rainy
• If they wear sunglasses → probably Sunny

Hidden states = actual weather (Sunny, Rainy, Cloudy)
Observations = what the friend wears (raincoat, sunglasses, jacket)

HMM in NLP — POS Tagging Connection

Hidden states = POS tags (Noun, Verb, Adjective…)
Observations = actual words ("dog", "runs", "fast"…)

We observe the words but we want to find the most likely sequence of POS tags.

3 Key Probabilities in HMM

ProbabilityMeaningExample
Transition P(tag₂ | tag₁)Probability of going from one tag to anotherP(Verb | Noun) = 0.6
Emission P(word | tag)Probability that a tag generates a specific wordP("dog" | Noun) = 0.3
Initial P(tag₁)Probability of a sentence starting with a tagP(start = Noun) = 0.5

🔢 Worked Numerical Example

Given:
P(Noun → Verb) = 0.6
P(Verb → Adjective) = 0.4

Find: Probability of the sequence Noun → Verb → Adjective

1

Write down the transition probabilities needed:
P(Noun → Verb) = 0.6
P(Verb → Adjective) = 0.4

2

For a sequence of transitions, multiply the probabilities:
P(sequence) = P(N→V) × P(V→JJ)

3

Calculate:
P = 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.24

Answer: P(Noun → Verb → Adjective) = 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.24

P(Noun → Adjective) = 0.2

Find: P(Noun → Verb → Noun) and P(Noun → Adjective)

P(N→V→N) = 0.5 × 0.3 = 0.15

🔍 Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD)

WSD is the task of automatically identifying which meaning (sense) of an ambiguous word is intended in a given context.

Problem: The word "bank" has at least 3 meanings.
Without context, a computer cannot know which one is meant.

Example — "Bank"

"He deposited money in the bank." → 🏦 Financial institution
"She sat by the river bank." → 🌊 Sloping land beside water
"The plane had to bank sharply." → ✈️ To tilt sideways (verb)

Why WSD Matters in Text Mining

ApplicationWhy WSD is Needed
Search Engines"Apple" query — fruit or company? Different results needed.
Machine TranslationTranslate "bank" correctly in Hindi (बैंक vs. किनारा)
ChatbotsUser says "I need to see a doctor" — doctor = physician, not PhD
Sentiment Analysis"The movie was sick!" — sick = bad or (slang) amazing?

3 WSD Approaches

① Supervised: Train a classifier on labeled examples where humans marked the correct sense. High accuracy but needs lots of labeled data.
② Dictionary-Based (Knowledge-Based): Use WordNet. Compare context words with dictionary definitions. Pick the sense whose definition overlaps most with the surrounding words. (Lesk Algorithm)
③ Unsupervised: Cluster occurrences of a word by their surrounding context words. No labels needed. Can discover new senses automatically.

Quick Comparison

ApproachInput NeededAccuracyBest When
SupervisedLabeled datasetHighestEnough labeled examples exist
Dictionary-BasedWordNet / dictionaryMediumNo labeled data available
UnsupervisedLarge unlabeled corpusLowerDiscovering new/unknown senses
